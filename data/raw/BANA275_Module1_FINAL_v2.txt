Challenge	1:	Ambiguity

"One	tries	to	be	as	informative	as	one	possibly	can,
and	gives	as	much	information	as	is	needed,	and	no	more."

—	Grice's	Maxim	of	Quantity

Challenge	2:	Sparsity

A	few	common	words	appear	very	frequently:

the,	of,	to,	and,	a,	in,	is...

Many	words	appear	very	rarely:

cornflakes,	mathematician,	fuzziness,	jumbling...

Over	1/3	of	words	in	any	corpus	occur	only	once

Zipf's	Law:	Regardless	of	data	size,

there	will	always	be	many	rare	words

Identifying	AI	Opportunities

Look	for	these	patterns:

— High-volume	repetitive	tasks	(emails,	documents,	support	tickets)

— Pattern	recognition	needs	(classification,	routing,	extraction)

— Human	bottlenecks	(waiting	for	review,	approval,	analysis)

— Inconsistent	quality	(different	people,	different	results)

— Time-sensitive	decisions	(real-time	recommendations)

Ask:	"Could	a	smart	intern	do	this	task?"
If	yes,	AI	probably	can	too

43

When	NOT	to	Use	AI

— Simple	rule-based	logic	works	fine

— No	training	data	available	(and	can't	create	it)

— Problem	requires	100%	accuracy

— High-stakes	decisions	(medical	diagnosis,	legal	judgments)

— Privacy	or	regulatory	constraints	make	it	impossible

— Cost	of	AI	exceeds	value	created

— Explainability	is	critical	but	AI	can't	provide	it

Why	Evaluation	Matters

"You	can't	improve	what	you	don't	measure"

— Production	requires	quantifiable	performance

— Baselines	establish	"good	enough"

— Testing	prevents	regressions

— Metrics	guide	iteration

— Evaluation	IS	the	product

47

500

Minimum	test	prompts	for	final	projects

This	separates	demos	from	deployable	systems

48

Building	an	Evaluation	Harness

— Test	Set:	Representative,	diverse,	adversarial	examples

— Metrics:	Task-appropriate	(accuracy,	F1,	BLEU,	human	evaluation)

— Baselines:	Simple	solutions	to	beat	(keyword	matching,	rules)

— Edge	Cases:	Where	systems	typically	fail	(sarcasm,	typos,	long
inputs)

— Consistency:	Same	input	→	same	output?

— Continuous	Testing:	Run	tests	on	every	code	change

49
